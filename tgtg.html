Too Good To Go rescues perfectly good food from being wasted, but their product ecosystem was starting to feel a little stale. That’s where Bagel comes in: a fresh, cohesive design system my team and I created to bring consistency, clarity, and flavor back to the interface. In this project, we baked a full set of tokens, components, and documentation to help the brand scale with confidence.

About the App

Too Good to Go is a global food-saving app with a growing product ecosystem.

Too Good To Go is a mission-driven company and mobile-first platform that helps people rescue surplus food from local restaurants, cafes, and grocery stores. Users can purchase discounted “surprise bags,” reducing waste while supporting their communities.

This project began in September 2025, and the interface reflects the app’s UI as it existed then.

TGTG Interface
Problem

However, as the app expanded, its product ecosystem grew rapidly, and most importantly, not always cohesively.

Foundational Inconsistencies

Core brand elements were applied inconsistently across screens, resulting in an experience that felt fragmented rather than cohesive.

Variations in UI Elements

UI elements appeared in multiple variations, suggesting components were frequently rebuilt instead of reused, leading to visual inconsistency.

Inefficient Workflows

Because styles and components weren’t standardized, designers and developers spent more time fixing inconsistencies instead of building new features.

Without a unified system, the product became harder to scale, harder to maintain, and more difficult to keep cohesive across surfaces.

This led to the formation of our problem statement.

And, a centralized design system emerged as the clear solution.

Foundation

To kickoff, we got started by deconstructing the building blocks of core screens.

Our first step was to break down the screens users interact with the most. Rather than looking at the product surface-level, we zoomed in, dissecting each page into its smallest parts to reveal the patterns, inconsistencies, and visual language.

By doing this, it served as the groundwork for defining the visual and structural groundwork that Bagel would eventually build upon.

Deconstruct a System

Kneading the foundational ingredients of the design system.

The foundations of Bagel establish the shared building blocks that bring consistency and cohesion across Too Good To Go. We definined clear rules for color, typography, iconography, layout, and spacing, creating a reliable baseline that teams can build from with confidence.

Semantic Variables

One challenge was defining color tokens, as the interface used inconsistent gray shades. We aligned on a primary gray and built a cohesive, semantic palette based on function rather than appearance—reducing guesswork and improving consistency across screens.

Color Accessibility

Another challenge was accessibility. Many color combinations failed WCAG contrast standards, so we refined the palette and established clear usage guidelines, documenting them to ensure accessibility is applied consistently as the system evolves.

Solution

A cohesive foundation that standardizes design decisions and drives visual consistency.

Principles

Shaping the core values that hold Bagel together: a system that reflects Too Good To Go’s mission.

Between defining foundations and building components, we worked on establishing principles to guide every decision. This process was a no-brainer: we knew our principles had to be shaped to reflect both product needs and Too Good To Go’s broader mission.

Below highlights the five principles that define Bagel:

Component

From ingredients to interface, we next baked out the component library.

Building a reusable component library to ensure consistency and speed across the product

We focused on translating our principles into reusable, flexible components. From avatars, input fields, navigation, to search bars, our components serve as building blocks of the product.

Previously, components were often recreated from scratch, leading to visual inconsistencies and inefficiencies in both design and development. By standardizing components and defining clear usage guidelines, we created a reusable system that streamlines workflows and ensures a more cohesive user experience as the product scales.

Flexible Components

Each component was designed to be robust, supporting use cases. A key example of this is the card component. I designed a single, robust horizontal card component that could flex across multiple contexts rather than a one-off solution.

Patterns

We also created patterns, reusable solutions for recurring UI structures, such as carousels, chip bars, and list layouts. They capture how components work together in real scenarios to reduce creating the same layouts and interactions.

Documentation

Every aspect of Bagel was documented to provide the team with a shared source of truth.

With the foundations and components in place, documentation became the bridge between the system and the people using it. We centralized everything in Zeroheight, creating a shared source of truth for designers, developers, and stakeholders.

Highlights

From documentation, socialization, to governance.

Getting Started

The introduction section lowers the barrier to adoption. These include step-by-step guides help designers and developers quickly understand why and how to use Bagel in their workflows, so that we can help team members start building independently and with confidence.

Clear Usage Rules

Rather than simply listing components, the documentation explains how and when to use them, outlines best practices and accessibility guidelines, and provides clear do’s and don’ts. These are applied to every foundation, component, and patterns so we leave no guesswork.

Anatomy and Variants

Each component is broken down into its core anatomy and supported by clearly defined variants, making it easier for designers and developers to understand how components are built and how they adapt across different use cases — a balance of customization and efficiency!

Governance Policies

To ensure Bagel remains sustainable over time, we established governance guidelines. These outline how to contribute and how changes are communicated, creating transparency around how the system evolves and establishing trust that Bagel is being actively maintained.

Why Bagel?

How Bagel elevates the product experience, from design to development.

Consistency at scale

Shared foundations, components, and patterns ensure experiences feel cohesive across every screen on the product and team.

Fast, efficient workflows

Reusable components and clear documentation reduce duplicated effort and speed up design and development.

Built-in sustainability

Accessible foundations and semantic guidelines help teams design inclusively while minimizing wasted work.

Reflection

The importance of designing with scalability in mind!

One of my biggest takeaways came from rethinking how I approached list items. At first, separate list item components were created for each use case across the interface, and I realized it wouldn’t scale as new content types were introduced.

I shifted toward a standardized list item component designed with flexibility at its core. This way, I created a solution that scales more gracefully and reduces the need for future rework.

In future projects, I hope to approach design with scalability in mind from the outset, thinking beyond immediate use cases to anticipate how design might evolve as products grow. This mindset will help me design systems (get it? 😉) that are more resilient, efficient, and scalable!
